1 1. Introduction

  • Context & Motivation: What the problem is, why it matters.
  • Research Questions & Objectives: State your RQs clearly; highlight objectives and scope.
  • Thesis Outline: Brief road map of chapters.

Tip: Keep this section concise. You can mention that more detailed reviews of certain concepts (e.g., network epidemiology, flight-based modeling) appear in Section 2.

2 2. Literature Review

  • Network Epidemiology & SIR Models: Summarize the theoretical foundations (SIR, network contagion).
  • COVID-19 & Global Mobility Networks (GMN): Prior research on flight data + COVID-19 spread.
  • Data Challenges in Flight-Based Epidemiology: Sources, ADS-B constraints, coverage, etc.

Tip: If your review is extremely detailed, consider sub-subsections, but ensure you keep it logically ordered. You’ll be referencing this in your methodology to show how you overcame or adapted to known challenges.

3 3. Methodology

  • Data Sources & Feasibility:
    • Where data come from (OpenSky, Eurocontrol), how/why you chose them, data cleaning steps.
  • Model Implementation:
    • SIR model design, equations (if needed), assumptions.
  • Simulation Steps & Parameter Choices:
    • Seeding rules, calibration decisions, load-factor sensitivities.
  • Statistical / Correlation Analysis:
    • How you plan to correlate arrival timings with excess mortality.

Tip: This section should be reproducible. Someone reading it should be able to grasp exactly how to replicate your pipeline.

4 4. Results & Analysis

  • Simulation Outcomes:
    • Main findings from your SIR model (e.g., arrival time estimates, R0, infection curves).
  • Comparison to Real Data:
    • Validation or partial validation with historical case timelines, partial flight volumes, etc.
  • Excess Mortality Correlation:
    • Results of Spearman’s rank (or other) correlation between arrival ranks and mortality.

Tip: Use clear figures (maps, Sankey diagrams, time-series plots) to illustrate your results.

5 5. Discussion

  • Did Early Arrival Matter?
    • Interpret whether your results support or refute the hypothesis that earlier arrival correlates with higher mortality.
  • Model Reliability & Data Biases
    • Reflect on coverage issues, assumptions (e.g., seat-capacity multiplication).
  • Limitations & Future Work
    • Where the approach might fail or be extended. Potential expansions (agent-based models, additional socioeconomic variables).

Tip: Some theses merge “Discussion” and “Conclusion” into one chapter, but having them separate often gives a sharper wrap-up.

6 6. Conclusion

  • Major Takeaways:
    • Summarize the key findings in one or two paragraphs.
  • Practical Implications:
    • Any direct recommendations for policymakers or future pandemic modeling?
  • Final Thoughts:
    • Possibly mention global expansions or how your method might be adapted for future epidemic scenarios.

7 7. References

  • Follow a consistent reference style (e.g., APA, Harvard, IEEE, etc.).
  • Make sure each reference is cited in-text.

8 Additional Notes

  • Acknowledgments: Perfect to put at the beginning or end, whichever your department’s format prefers.
  • Appendices: If you have large tables, code, or raw data examples, place them in an appendix. That keeps the main text lean.
  • For a Bachelor thesis, ensure your content is thorough but not labyrinthine. Clarity and coherence are paramount.
